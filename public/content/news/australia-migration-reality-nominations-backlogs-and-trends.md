## State and Territory Nominations (2025–26 Program Year)

The Department of Home Affairs has published the number of Expressions of Interest (EOIs) that received nominations from state and territory governments between 1 July 2025 and 31 March 2026, which covers information about both Subclass 190 (Skilled Nominated visa) and Subclass 491 (Skilled Work Regional (Provisional) visa).

## Key Insights

Victoria has already issued 1,893 nominations out of 2,700 for Subclass 190, which shows that fewer spots are available for the rest of the year.

Queensland and South Australia are progressing steadily, with around half of their allocations already used.

New South Wales continues to dominate in volume, with 1,332 places for 190 and 1,031 places for 491 nominations already issued.

Western Australia is sitting closer to the halfway mark across both visas, which indicates it still has a fair number of places left to issue.

The Northern Territory is moving fast, with 640 out of 850 places already used for subclass 190.


## Updates Across Other Skilled Migration and Employer-Sponsored Visas

Beyond state nominations, the Department’s latest release also offers a detailed view of visa backlogs across key skilled migration and employer-sponsored pathways. This includes occupation-wise applications on hand for Subclass 189, 190, and 491 as of 31 December 2025, along with the number of primary applicants on hand for Subclass 186 and Subclass 482 as of 28 February 2026.

You can access the complete backlog data released by the Department of Home Affairs here:  
https://www.homeaffairs.gov.au/foi/files/data-request-2026/da-260100049-document-released.PDF

This data confirms that Australia’s migration program is no longer driven by a linear points-based system but by targeted selection aligned with workforce demand and evolving Australia PR requirements. Backlogs across Subclass 189, 190, and 491 are heavily concentrated in high-supply occupations.

In contrast, Subclass 186 and 482 continue to reflect demand-led processing, where the employer's needs directly influence outcomes. The implication is clear: applicants positioned within priority occupations or employer-sponsored pathways are progressing with greater certainty, while others face prolonged timelines in increasingly competitive pools.
